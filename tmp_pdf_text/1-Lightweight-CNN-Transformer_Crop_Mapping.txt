ABSTRACT
Deep learning approaches have provided state-of-the-art performance in crop mapping. Recently, several studies 
have combined the strengths of two dominant deep learning architectures, Convolutional Neural Networks 
(CNNs) and Transformers, to classify crops using remote sensing images. Despite their success, many of these 
models utilize patch-based methods that require extensive data labeling, as each sample contains multiple pixels 
with corresponding labels. This leads to higher costs in data preparation and processing. Moreover, previous 
methods rarely considered the impact of missing values caused by clouds and no-observations in remote sensing 
data. Therefore, this study proposes a lightweight multi-stage CNN-Transformer network (MCTNet) for pixel- 
based crop mapping using time-series Sentinel-2 imagery. MCTNet consists of several successive modules, 
each containing a CNN sub-module and a Transformer sub-module to extract important features from the images, 
respectively. An attention-based learnable positional encoding (ALPE) module is designed in the Transformer 
sub-module to capture the complex temporal relations in the time-series data with different missing rates. 
Arkansas and California in the U.S. are selected to evaluate the model. Experimental results show that the 
MCTNet has a lightweight advantage with the fewest parameters and memory usage while achieving the superior 
performance compared to eight advanced models. Specifically, MCTNet obtained an overall accuracy (OA) of 
0.968, a kappa coefficient (Kappa) of 0.951, and a macro-averaged F1 score (F1) of 0.933 in Arkansas, and an OA 
of 0.852, a Kappa of 0.806, and an F1 score of 0.829 in California. The results highlight the importance of each 
component of the model, particularly the ALPE module, which enhanced the Kappa of MCTNet by 4.2% in 
Arkansas and improved the model ’ s robustness to missing values in remote sensing data. Additionally, visuali -
zation results demonstrated that the features extracted from CNN and Transformer sub-modules are comple -
mentary, explaining the effectiveness of the MCTNet.
1. Introduction
Reliable crop maps are essential for monitoring agricultural land use 
and aiding decision-makers in issuing effective policies to guide sus -
tainable agricultural development ( Bargiel, 2017 ). Remote sensing has 
become the primary means of crop mapping due to its extensive range of 
observation, regular revisit time, and low cost. Especially, after the 
successful launch of Sentinel-2 satellites, it is easier to access optical 
data with fine temporal and spatial resolutions. The time-series optical 
imagery characterizes the dynamic growth patterns of crops, providing 
additional valuable information for crop classification ( Ashourloo et al., 
2019; Yuan et al., 2022 ).
Traditional crop mapping approaches using time-series remote 
sensing data can be broadly categorized into three types: threshold- 
based, statistics-based, and machine learning methods. Threshold- 
based methods primarily consider the optimal seasonal threshold to 
separate different crop types based on phenological characteristics and 
expert experience ( Chen et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2022 ). Statistics- 
based approaches focus on statistically linking phenology and bio -
physical changes during crop growth by constructing predefined math -
ematical functions or models ( Achanccaray et al., 2017; Asad and Bais, 
2020 ). However, the aforementioned approaches have limited modeling

( Blickensd ¨orfer et al., 2022; Qiu et al., 2018 ). Machine learning has the 
advantage of effectively utilizing a wider range of features. Various 
machine learning models have been successfully applied to crop map -
ping, such as support vector machine (SVM) and random forest (RF) 
( Feng et al., 2019; Maponya et al., 2020 ). However, several drawbacks 
exist in traditional machine learning models. First, feature engineering is 
heavily relied upon to select important information and remove 
redundant information ( Xu et al., 2020 ). Second, time-series spectral 
features are often treated as a collection of independent features, 
ignoring the temporal correlations ( Inglada et al., 2017; Interdonato 
et al., 2019 ).
With the fast development of artificial intelligence, deep learning 
models have exhibited immense potential in crop mapping ( Zhong et al., 
2019 ). Superior to traditional machine learning methods, deep learning 
can automatically learn discriminating features from complex informa -
tion, eliminating the effort to select features manually ( Schmidhuber, 
2015 ). Among the various deep learning approaches, convolutional 
neural networks (CNNs), recurrent neural networks (RNNs), and 
Transformers have been widely used to classify crops ( Ru ß wurm and 
K ¨orner, 2020 ). CNNs apply convolution operations to extract features. 
Since convolution has a fixed kernel size which leads to a local receptive 
field, CNNs are highly efficient at extracting local information but have 
insufficient ability to capture global information ( Wang et al., 2022a ). 
RNNs are specifically designed for time-series data modeling, which 
sequentially processes each time step through recurrent cells. In 
particular, long short-term memory (LSTM) is a widely used RNN 
method for land cover classifications using time-series images ( Sun 
et al., 2019 ). Unlike RNNs, Transformers utilize the multi-head self- 
attention mechanism and positional encoding to process all time steps of 
sequential data simultaneously ( Vaswani et al., 2017 ). This mechanism 
is inherently flexible and can understand the relationships between 
distant parts of the input sequence to obtain both local and global in -
formation. Different from the fixed receptive field of CNNs, Trans -
formers can adapt their attention dynamically and capture long-range 
dependencies in sequential data, which is essential for understanding 
the overall temporal dynamics and growth patterns of crops across an 
entire season. For example, Yuan and Lin (2021) proposed a 
Transformer-based model for crop mapping and achieved better per -
formance than RNNs. Additionally, several studies have proposed more 
efficient models based on Transformers for mining time-series images, 
such as temporal attention encoder (TAE) ( Garnot et al., 2020 ) and 
lightweight temporal attention encoder (LTAE) ( Garnot and Landrieu, 
2020 ).
Since CNNs excel at extracting local features while Transformers are 
adept at capturing long-term dependencies, several recent studies have 
combined the strengths of both approaches to enhance crop mapping 
performance ( Liu et al., 2022; Niu et al., 2022; Tang et al., 2024; Wang 
et al., 2022a; Xiang et al., 2023 ). However, these hybrid models have 
primarily focused on patch-based crop mapping in small regions. 
Although patch-based models can utilize additional spatial information 
compared to pixel-based models, the cost of patch-based sampling is 
more expensive ( Feng et al., 2023 ), limiting their application in large 
areas. Moreover, most of these models used an encoder-decoder struc -
ture commonly applied in language translation and segmentation tasks. 
The encoder-decoder structures can increase the volume of the model, 
making it unsuitable for lightweight applications. In contrast, pixel- 
based crop mapping is a typical classification problem that only re -
quires an encoder to extract features for classification ( Li et al., 2020 ). 
Zhang et al. (2023) integrated a CNN and a modified Transformer to 
design a global – local temporal attention encoder (GL-TAE) for pixel- 
based crop mapping. In their approach, time-series multi-spectral data 
is transformed into a 2D matrix, enabling the CNN to extract local 
temporal patterns. These local features are then merged with features 
extracted by the Transformer to enhance crop mapping. The results 
demonstrate that this hybrid model significantly outperforms both the 
CNN and the Transformer models.
However, previous CNN-Transformer models simply integrated fea -
tures extracted from CNN and Transformer while giving limited 
consideration to missing data. Missing values in time-series images are 
often inevitable due to factors such as cloud cover and non-observation. 
These missing values can disrupt the inherent temporal patterns of 
crops, potentially affecting the performance of models, especially those 
that consider sequential information, such as Transformers. Although 
Ru ß wurm and K ¨orner (2020) found that the self-attention mechanism of 
Transformers reduces the impact of missing values in time-series remote 
sensing data for crop mapping to a certain extent, it is not specifically 
designed to address missing values, and thus performance improve -
ments are necessary. Several studies have applied interpolation tech -
niques to eliminate missing values ( Wang et al., 2022b; You et al., 2021 ). 
However, performing interpolation demands extensive computational 
resources for multi-bands in large temporal-spatial scales. Moreover, 
there remains an inherent uncertainty regarding the fidelity of the 
reconstructed data to the actual values, which raises concerns about the 
reliability of interpolation in applications where the accuracy of tem -
poral information is paramount.
Therefore, we propose a lightweight multi-stage CNN-Transformer 
network (MCTNet) for pixel-based crop mapping using time-series 
sentinel-2 imagery. MCTNet consists of several successive CNN- 
Transformer fusion (CTFusion) modules with pooling operations and a 
multilayer perceptron (MLP) classifier. Each CTFusion has a CNN sub- 
module and a Transformer sub-module. The CNN sub-module is 
designed with a 1D convolutional network to extract local spectral in -
formation along the time dimension. The Transformer sub-module is 
designed based on the encoder of the original Transformer to capture the 
long-range dependencies of the time series data and extract the features 
from a global perspective. Besides, we designed an attention-based 
learnable positional encoding module (ALPE) in the first Transformer 
sub-module to capture the temporal correlation in the time series data 
under different missing rates. We selected California and Arkansas in the 
U.S. as study areas to verify the effectiveness of the model with distinct 
planting structures. At the same time, eight advanced models were 
selected for comparative verification. The main contributions of our 
research are:
(1) We propose the MCTNet that integrates the advantages of the 
CNN and Transformer with a lightweight architecture for pixel-based 
crop mapping.
(2) ALPE is designed to reduce the impact of missing values and 
provide reasonable positional encoding, which can improve the model ’ s 
ability to handle time-series data under complex missing conditions.
(3) Compared with various state-of-the-art models, the proposed 
model exhibits superior performance in key areas, including parameter 
size, classification accuracy, and the quality of mapping results.
2. Materials and methods
2.1. Study area
Two states in the U.S. were chosen as the study areas, including 
California and Arkansas ( Fig. 1 (a)). California has an extremely diverse 
natural landscape, including grasslands, deserts, rainforests, mountains, 
and oceans. The agricultural system in this state is quite complex, with 
small plots and a variety of crop types ( Zhong et al., 2011 ). Arkansas is 
an important agricultural state in the U.S. with over 40 % of the land 
dedicated to agricultural production ( Martin et al., 2021 ). It is a major 
producer of crops such as rice, soybeans, corn, cotton, and wheat, which 
are extensively cultivated on large farms. Due to the differences in major 
crops and cultivation methods between California and Arkansas, the 
crop mapping models can be thoroughly validated. Besides, two distinct 
mapping areas were selected in each state to assess the practical appli -
cation capability of the models ( Fig. 1 (b) and Fig. 1 (c)). These areas 
were selected for their considerable geographical separation and

2.2. Datasets
Three types of datasets were collected in this study, including ground 
truth of crop type, land cover data, and remote sensing images. Table 1
provides a summary for each data source and the detailed descriptions 
are in the following sections.
2.2.1. Ground truth
Cropland Data Layer Data (CDL) is one of the most widely used crop 
maps published by the U.S. Department of Agriculture (USDA). CDL 
describes the distribution of more than 100 crops in the U.S., completely 
covering Arkansas and California. It has an annual update frequency, 
high spatial resolution (30 m), and high classification accuracy ( Boryan 
et al., 2011 ). Moreover, it offers a confidence layer that indicates the 
predicted confidence level for each pixel. Therefore, this study used the 
CDL data to generate labeled samples for model training and testing.
2.2.2. ESA WorldCover 2021
The ESA WorldCover 2021 is a comprehensive global land cover map 
for 2021, offering a fine spatial resolution of 10 m. This pioneering 
product, derived from Sentinel-1 and Sentinel-2 data, provides a 
detailed classification of the earth ’ s surface into 11 land cover classes. 
Since this study focuses on crop mapping, ESA WorldCover 2021 was 
used to mask non-cropland areas, thereby streamlining sample collec -
tion and practical mapping applications.
2.2.3. Remote sensing images
Sentinel-2 is a multi-spectral imaging system provided by the Euro -
pean Space Agency (ESA) and designed for high-resolution imaging of 
the Earth ’ s surface. It comprises two satellites that can be combined to 
provide full coverage of the Earth ’ s surface every five days. Sentinel-2 
has 13 bands ranging from the visible to the shortwave infrared, with 
spatial resolutions varying up to a maximum of 10 m ( Table 1 ). Among 
these bands, Band 1, Band 9, and Band 10 have an inadequate resolution 
(60 m) and contribute less to the fine classification of crops; thus, we 
selected the remaining ten bands as spectral features, including three 
visible bands, one near-infrared (NIR) band, four red-edge bands, and 
two shortwave infrared (SWIR) bands. Besides, Sentinel-2 Level-2A 
surface reflectance images (bottom-of-atmosphere) were applied in this 
study because these images are atmospherically corrected and orthor -
ectified, contributing to more accurate and reliable monitoring of agri -
cultural conditions.
2.2.4. Data preprocessing
For remote sensing observations, we collected all Sentinel-2 images 
over the study areas from the year 2021. Subsequently, we eliminated 
cloud-affected pixels and computed the median value of the remaining 
observations at ten-day intervals, resulting in a total of 36 temporal 
sequences. It is noted that there are still missing data in the sequences, 
and we used 0 to mark the missing data. We used the CDL map from 
2021 to collect labeled crop samples. In particular, we set a 95 % con -
fidence to filter the CDL map to improve the quality of sampling and 
used the ESA WorldCover 2021 to mask non-cropland areas. Then, we 
randomly sampled 10,000 points in each study area and extracted the 
corresponding crop type from the CDL map, as well as the associated 
spectral features from the time-series images. As a result, each labeled 
sample has 360 spectral features, consisting of 10 spectral bands with 36 
temporal observations. The distribution of the crop samples is shown in 
Table 2 . Specifically, crop types that constitute less than 5 % of the total 
number of samples were merged into a category labeled as “ others ” . The 
NDVI time-series curve of each crop is generated by averaging the NDVI 
values of all the corresponding crop samples ( Fig. 2 ). All the necessary 
steps for data collection and preprocessing were executed using the 
Google Earth Engine.

2.3.2. CNN sub-module and MLP classifier
The CNN sub-module is designed with a very simple structure 
( Fig. 6 ). It consists of two consecutive 1D convolution layers that operate 
along the time dimension of the input. Each convolution layer is fol -
lowed by a batch normalization layer. The output of the two layers is 
processed by a ReLU activation function. Besides, a connection directly 
skips over the two convolutional layers and adds the input of the sub- 
module to the output of the two layers. This shortcut connection can 
enable the gradient to flow more easily during training and helps to 
avoid the vanishing gradient problem. The MLP classifier is made up of a 
linear layer with a Softmax activation. The input of the MLP is the 
extracted features from the last CTFusion module. Specifically, the 
output of the last CTFusion module is processed by a global max pooling 
operation along the channel dimension to transform its form into a one- 
dimensional vector. Then the vector is fed to the MLP classifier to predict 
the crop type.
2.4. Experimental setting
There are 10,000 samples in Arkansas and California, respectively. 
For each state, the samples were split into training, validation, and 
testing sets. Specifically, 300 samples per crop type were randomly 
selected to compose the training and validation datasets (with a rate of 
8:2), and the rest samples were used for testing. The details of the sample 
partition are shown in Table 2 . The training samples were used to train 
the models, and the validation samples were used for selecting hyper -
parameters, including the number of stages (n_stage), the number of 
heads (n_head) in the Transformer sub-module, the kernel size of the 
CNN sub-module, the learning rate (lr), and the optimizer. To fully 
assess the effectiveness of the MCTNet, we compared it with eight 
advanced models, including SVM, RF, LSTM, Transformer, Residual 
Neural Network (ResNet), TAE, LTAE, and GL-TAE. Particularly, ResNet 
is a deep CNN with many variants, and this study adopted the ResNet- 
18, which has 18 convolutional layers. Since the original Transformer 
is not suitable for classification tasks, this study adopted the modified 
Transformer model of Ru ß wurm and K ¨orner (2020) , which only contains 
the encoder part of the original Transformer.
All models were implemented in a Python environment, in which 
SVM and RF were constructed with the Scikit-Learn library, and the deep 
learning models were constructed with the PyTorch library. The models 
all ran on a workstation with an Intel I7-12900 CPU, 64 GB RAM, and an 
NVIDIA RTX3090 24G GPU. In the training process, the batch size and 
number of epochs of the deep learning models were set as 32 and 200, 
respectively. The hyperparameters of the models for tuning, their can -
didates, and their final value in each study area are listed in Table 3 . The 
other hyperparameters of the models were selected with default values.
To quantitatively evaluate the models, the confusion matrix was first 
computed to show the distribution of all the predicted responses. Then,

metric for evaluating a model ’ s effectiveness in imbalanced datasets. For 
the sake of brevity, this metric will be referred to as “ F1 ″ in subsequent 
discussions. The formulas of the three metrics are shown in Eq. (4)-(6).

3. Results
3.1. Ablation experiment
To validate the effectiveness of the components, we assessed the 
impact of the ALPE module, CNN sub-module, and Transformer sub- 
module on the performance of the MCTNet. Specifically, we removed 
the three modules separately to build three new comparison models, 
namely MCTNet_noALPE, MCTNet_noCnn, and MCTNet_noTrans. It is 
worth noting that the MCTNet_noALPE model used the absolute posi -
tional encoding of the original Transformer. Besides, we also removed 
the data missing mask of the ALPE to construct MCTNet_noMask to 
assess its effectiveness.
Table 4 shows the overall performance of the models in the two study 
areas. It is clear that the MCTNet outperformed the other models 
significantly in both regions, illustrating that each module contributes to 
improving classification performance. In Arkansas, the MCTNet 
obtained the best performance with 0.968 OA, 0.951 Kappa, and 0.933 
F1, followed by MCTNet_noMask. The MCTNet_noALPE and MCTNet_ -
noCnn performed similarly, with an F1 that is approximately 4 % less 
than that of MCTNet. The MCTNet_noTrans produced the lowest accu -
racy, indicating that the Transformer sub-module had the greatest 
impact on the accuracy of the model. In California, the accuracy of the 
models decreased significantly compared to the results in Arkansas. This 
may be attributed to the small crop plots and numerous types of crops in 
California, which increase the difficulty of classification. The MCTNet 
still exhibited the best performance, followed by the MCTNet_noMask, 
MCTNet_noALPE, MCTNet_noCnn, and MCTNet_noTrans.
3.2. Model performance comparison
The results of the classification accuracy of the nine models in the 
two regions are displayed in Table 5 . A major finding is that the MCTNet 
achieved the best performance while the LSTM, SVM, and TAE yielded 
the worst results in both regions. LSTM relies on sequential data to learn 
temporal dynamics, but missing data can impact its ability to capture 
temporal dependencies. SVM is also sensitive to noise and outliers in the 
dataset ( Sabzekar and Hasheminejad, 2021 ). RF yielded significantly 
superior accuracy compared to LSTM, SVM, and TAE. This is because RF 
can exhibit robustness through majority voting with its numerous trees. 
Apart from the proposed MCTNet, the GL-TAE also performed well in 
both study areas. This performance can be attributed to the fact that 
both MCTNet and GL-TAE integrate CNN and self-attention architec -
tures. Despite their differing self-attention mechanisms, this integration 
facilitates the advantageous combination of global and local attention, 
which is critical for effective crop mapping. Generally, the MCTNet 
outperformed other comparison models remarkably in all metrics.

3.4. Cross-temporal performance
In real applications, it is often impossible to collect training data for 
specific years, making the evaluation of the transferability and gener -
alizability of models crucial. To explore cross-temporal performance, we 
conducted an additional experiment to assess the performance of models 
across different years. Specifically, we collected 5000 samples from each 
study area in 2020 and 2022, and tested the performance of models 
trained on 2021 data using these new datasets. The results, shown in 
Fig. 11 , indicate that the proposed MCTNet consistently outperforms 
other models, achieving the highest scores in most scenarios, particu -
larly in Arkansas 2020 and 2022, and California 2020. Although 
MCTNet ranks second to RF in California 2022, it remains highly 
competitive. In contrast, the comparison models exhibit significant 
performance variations across different scenarios. For example, while 
the RF model maintains good performance across years in California, it 
shows inferior performance in Arkansas. This indicates that the pro -
posed model effectively and precisely captures the temporal patterns of 
crops, demonstrating superior robustness and stability in handling un -
seen temporal data.
4. Discussion
4.1. Model interpretation
The results in Section 3 substantiated the efficacy of the MCTNet by 
leveraging the integration of the CNN and the Transformer to enhance 
feature extraction capabilities. To further investigate the contribution of 
the CNN and Transformer sub-modules in the proposed model, the 
gradient-weighted class activation mapping (Grad-CAM) ( Selvaraju 
et al., 2017 ) was applied to visualize the extracted features by the sub- 
modules. Specifically, the Grad-CAM on the last convolutional layer of 
the CNN sub-module and the last normalized layer of the Transformer 
sub-module were selected for visualization. To explore the effects of the 
multiple stages in the MCTNet, the visualization results were also pro -
duced for each stage.
Fig. 12 (a1)   (a3) demonstrate the Grad-CAM results of CNN sub- 
modules using the corn samples in Arkansas. In the spectral dimen -
sion, the red edge and NIR bands, known to be sensitive to the physio -
logical state of plants ( Sharifi, 2020 ), exhibit significant importance. In 
the temporal domain, the features near the 180 DOY obtain more 
attention. This period corresponds to the tasseling and silking stages of 
corn, during which the plants undergo significant structural and color 
changes. From a remote sensing perspective, these developmental stages 
are characterized by notable alterations in the spectral reflectance of the 
corn canopy, making them easily detectable. Additionally, as illustrated 
in Fig. 2 , the occurrence of these stages in corn is significantly earlier 
compared to other crops. Consequently, the spectral features during 
these stages are important for crop identification and thus receive 
heightened attention. Moreover, we found that the important features 
become more concentrated from the first stage to the last stage. This is 
because the features learned by deep CNN models exhibit hierarchical 
characteristics, that is, the shallow layers learn abstract features while 
the deeper layers learn specific and invariant features that serve for 
classification ( Zeiler and Fergus, 2013 ). Fig. 12 (c1)   (c3) also shows a 
gradual concentration phenomenon in California with grape samples. 
Different from corn, the important features of grapes are a little bit away 
from the peak value date ( Fig. 2 ). Deep learning models are often viewed 
as ’ black boxes ’ with limited interpretability, and the optimal combi -
nation of features might not include the peak date, especially when the 
peak is not markedly evident. In contrast, the Grad-CAM results of 
Transformer sub-modules ( Fig. 12 (b1)   (b3) and Fig. 12 (d1)   (d3)) 
are different from those of the CNN sub-modules. The important features 
of Transformer sub-modules become more scattered along the temporal 
domain from the first stage to the last stage. This is because CNN models

4.2. The impact of missing data on model performance
To investigate the impact of varying degrees of missingness in time- 
series data on model accuracy, we calculated the missing rates for each 
sample in the testing dataset in Arkansas and then assessed the robust -
ness of MCTNet and MCTNet_noALPE models using samples with 
missing rates less than 10 %, 15 %, 20 %, 25 %, 30 %, 35 %, and 40 % 
respectively. We believe it is important to maintain realistic observa -
tions, so we did not refer to previous studies ( Wang et al., 2022b; You 
et al., 2021 ) using linear interpolation and Savitzky-Golay smoothing to 
process time-series data to eliminate missing values. To verify whether 
these steps are necessary, we used the data that underwent interpolation 
and smoothing to generate two new models (named MCTNet1 and 
MCTNet_noALPE1). The model performance and the distribution of 
missing rates of data are presented in Fig. 15 . It is obvious that the data 
with less than 10 % missingness constituted less than 20 % of the total, 
which implies that the vast majority of the data contained missing values 
with different rates. As the missing rate increased, the accuracy of all 
models gradually declined. The MCTNet achieved the best performance 
at every rate, and its OA decreased slightly, from 0.973 to 0.968. By 
contrast, the OA of MCTNet_noALPE dropped from 0.963 to 0.950. This 
demonstrates that MCTNet, owing to the contribution of ALPE, was less 
sensitive to data missingness than MCTNet_noALPE.
It is noteworthy that MCTNet significantly outperformed MCTNet1 
across all missing scenarios, indicating that interpolation and smoothing 
might alter the original distribution of the data, leading to a reduction in 
model accuracy. In contrast, the accuracy of MCTNet_noALPE1 was 
persistently higher than that of MCTNet_noALPE, especially when data 
missingness is substantial. This discrepancy arose because

4.3. Limitations and future work
Although the proposed model achieved superior performance in crop 
mapping, there are several limitations for improvement. First, the 
MCTNet model may produce salt-and-pepper noise in classification re -
sults. This noise results from pixel-wise decision-making, which can be 
pronounced in heterogeneous, small, and broken agricultural landscapes 
(like California ( Fig. 10 )). Further studies will consider the spatial in -
formation of the input pixel to improve mapping accuracy. For example, 
as suggested by Interdonato et al. (2019) , incorporating unlabeled pixels 
surrounding the input pixel in the modeling could enhance performance. 
Another limitation of our study is the exclusive use of optical data, while 
recent studies have shown the efficiency of SAR and hyperspectral data 
in crop monitoring and classification ( Farmonov et al., 2023; Sharifi and 
Hosseingholizadeh, 2020 ). We plan to integrate multi-modal remote 
sensing data in future research to enhance the robustness and accuracy 
of crop type classification. Lastly, although the current study areas cover 
two U.S. states with unique agricultural characteristics, it remains 
imperative to explore the performance of the proposed model in various 
other countries and regions.
5. Conclusions
This study proposed a lightweight CNN-Transformer network 
MCTNet for pixel-based crop mapping using time-series Sentinel-2 im -
ages. MCTNet effectively harnesses the advantages of both CNN and 
Transformer to obtain a comprehensive representation of crop growth 
patterns. Compared with eight advanced models, the MCTNet exhibited 
better performance in most crop classification cases with the fewest 
number of parameters. The evaluation of the impact of data missing on 
the models demonstrated that the proposed ALPE made the MCTNet less 
sensitive to the missing values and achieved superior performance 
without data interpolation and smoothing. Moreover, MCTNet achieved 
superior performance in cross-temporal scenarios, indicating its high 
potential in practical applications. Further work aims to improve the 
model ’ s productivity and generalizability across different regions.
